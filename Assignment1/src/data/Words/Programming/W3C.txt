class="url"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.w3.org">www<wbr />.w3<wbr />.org</a></span></td>/n</tr>/n</table>/n<p>The <b>World Wide Web Consortium</b> (<b>W3C</b>) is the main international <a href="/wiki/Standards_organization" title="Standards organization">standards organization</a> for the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> (abbreviated WWW or W3).</p>/n<p>Founded and currently led by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-consortium_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-consortium-3">[3]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Consortium" title="Consortium">consortium</a> is made up of member organizations which maintain full-time staff for the purpose of working together in the development of standards for the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>. As of 24 September 2017<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) has 463 members.<sup id="cite_ref-MembersList_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MembersList-2">[2]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The W3C also engages in education and outreach, develops software and serves as an open <a href="/wiki/Internet_forum" title="Internet forum">forum</a> for discussion about the Web.</p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) was founded by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a> after he left the European Organization for Nuclear Research (<a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a>) (Conseil Européen pour la Recherche Nucléaire) in October, 1994. It was founded at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Computer_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Laboratory for Computer Science">Laboratory for Computer Science</a> (MIT/LCS) with support from the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> and the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (<a href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">DARPA</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-consortium_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-consortium-3">[3]</a></sup> which had pioneered the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> and its predecessor <a href="/wiki/ARPANET" title="ARPANET">ARPANET</a>.</p>/n<p>The organization tries to foster compatibility and agreement among industry members in the adoption of new standards defined by the W3C. Incompatible versions of HTML are offered by different vendors, causing inconsistency in how web pages are displayed. The consortium tries to get all those vendors to implement a set of core principles and components which are chosen by the consortium.</p>/n<p>It was originally intended that CERN host the European branch of W3C; however, CERN wished to focus on particle physics, not information technology. In April 1995, the <a href="/wiki/French_Institute_for_Research_in_Computer_Science_and_Automation" title="French Institute for Research in Computer Science and Automation">French Institute for Research in Computer Science and Automation</a> (INRIA) became the European host of W3C, with <a href="/wiki/Keio_University" title="Keio University">Keio University</a> Research Institute at SFC (KRIS) becoming the Asian host in September 1996<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>. Starting in 1997, W3C created regional offices around the world. As of September 2009, it had eighteen World Offices covering Australia, the Benelux countries (Netherlands, Luxembourg, and Belgium), Brazil, China, Finland, Germany, Austria, Greece, Hong Kong, Hungary, India, Israel, Italy, South Korea, Morocco, South Africa, Spain, Sweden, and, as of 2016, the United Kingdom and Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Offices_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Offices-5">[5]</a></sup></p>/n<p>In October 2012, W3C convened a community of major web players and publishers to establish a <a href="/wiki/MediaWiki" title="MediaWiki">MediaWiki</a> wiki that seeks to document open web standards called the <a href="/wiki/WebPlatform" title="WebPlatform">WebPlatform</a> and WebPlatform Docs.</p>/n<p>In January 2013, <a href="/wiki/Beihang_University" title="Beihang University">Beihang University</a> became the Chinese host.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specification_maturation">Specification maturation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Specification maturation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Sometimes, when a specification becomes too large, it is split into independent modules which can mature at their own pace. Subsequent editions of a module or specification are known as levels and are denoted by the first integer in the title (e.g. CSS3 = Level 3). Subsequent revisions on each level are denoted by an integer following a decimal point (e.g. CSS2.1 = Revision 1).</p>/n<p>The W3C standard formation process is defined within the W3C process document, outlining four maturity levels through which each new standard or recommendation must progress.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span id="Working_draft_(WD)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Working_draft_.28WD.29">Working draft (WD)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Working draft (WD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>After enough content has been gathered from 'editor drafts' and discussion, it may be published as a working draft (WD) for review by the community. A WD document is the first form of a standard that is publicly available. Commentary by virtually anyone is accepted, though no promises are made with regard to action on any particular element commented upon.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<p>At this stage, the standard document may have significant differences from its final form. As such, anyone who implements WD standards should be ready to significantly modify their implementations as the standard matures.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span id="Candidate_recommendation_(CR)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Candidate_recommendation_.28CR.29">Candidate recommendation (CR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Candidate recommendation (CR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>A candidate recommendation is a version of a standard that is more mature than the WD. At this point, the group responsible for the standard is satisfied that the standard meets its goal. The purpose of the CR is to elicit aid from the development community as to how implementable the standard is.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The standard document may change further, but at this point, significant features are mostly decided. The design of those features can still change due to feedback from implementors.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span id="Proposed_recommendation_(PR)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_recommendation_.28PR.29">Proposed recommendation (PR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proposed recommendation (PR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>A proposed recommendation is the version of a standard that has passed the prior two levels. The users of the standard provide input. At this stage, the document is submitted to the W3C Advisory Council for final approval.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<p>While this step is important, it rarely causes any significant changes to a standard as it passes to the next phase.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Both candidates and proposals may enter "last call" to signal that any further feedback must be provided.</p>/n<h3><span id="W3C_recommendation_(REC)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="W3C_recommendation_.28REC.29">W3C recommendation (REC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: W3C recommendation (REC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>This is the most mature stage of development. At this point, the standard has undergone extensive review and testing, under both theoretical and practical conditions. The standard is now endorsed by the W3C, indicating its readiness for deployment to the public, and encouraging more widespread support among implementors and authors.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Recommendations can sometimes be implemented incorrectly, partially, or not at all, but many standards define two or more levels of conformance that developers must follow if they wish to label their product as W3C-compliant.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_revisions">Later revisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Later revisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>A recommendation may be updated or extended by separately-published, non-technical errata or editor drafts until sufficient substantial edits accumulate for producing a new edition or level of the recommendation. Additionally, the W3C publishes various kinds of informative notes which are to be used as references.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Certification">Certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Society" title="Internet Society">ISOC</a> and other international standards bodies, the W3C does not have a certification program. The W3C has decided, for now, that it is not suitable to start such a program, owing to the risk of creating more drawbacks for the community than benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-W3CDevProc_6-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3CDevProc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The Consortium is jointly administered by the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Computer_Science_and_Artificial_Intelligence_Laboratory" title="MIT Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory">MIT Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory</a> (CSAIL, located in <a href="/wiki/Stata_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Stata Center">Stata Center</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>) in the USA, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Research_Consortium_for_Informatics_and_Mathematics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="European Research Consortium for Informatics and Mathematics (page does not exist)">European Research Consortium for Informatics and Mathematics</a> (ERCIM) (in <a href="/wiki/Sophia_Antipolis" title="Sophia Antipolis">Sophia Antipolis</a>, France), <a href="/wiki/Keio_University" title="Keio University">Keio University</a> (in Japan) and <a href="/wiki/Beihang_University" title="Beihang University">Beihang University</a> (in China).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The W3C also has World Offices in sixteen regions around the world. The W3C Offices work with their regional web communities to promote W3C technologies in local languages, broaden the W3C's geographical base and encourage international participation in W3C Activities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>The W3C has a staff team of 70–80 worldwide as of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> W3C is run by a management team which allocates resources and designs strategy, led by CEO <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeffrey_Jaffe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jeffrey Jaffe (page does not exist)">Jeffrey Jaffe</a> (as of March 2010),<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> former CTO of <a href="/wiki/Novell" title="Novell">Novell</a>. It also includes an advisory board which supports in strategy and legal matters and helps resolve conflicts.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The majority of standardization work is done by external experts in the W3C's various working groups.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership">Membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The Consortium is governed by its membership. The list of members is available to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-MembersList_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MembersList-2">[2]</a></sup> Members include businesses, nonprofit organizations, universities, governmental entities, and individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-membershipreq_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-membershipreq-12">[12]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Membership requirements are transparent except for one requirement: An application for membership must be reviewed and approved by the W3C. Many guidelines and requirements are stated in detail, but there is no final guideline about the process or standards by which membership might be finally approved or denied.<sup id="cite_ref-membershipjoin_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-membershipjoin-13">[13]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The cost of membership is given on a sliding scale, depending on the character of the organization applying and the country in which it is located.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Countries are categorized by the World Bank's most recent grouping by GNI ("Gross National Income") per capita.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Web_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" the EME specification as a Recommendation, leading to <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Frontier_Foundation" title="Electronic Frontier Foundation">Electronic Frontier Foundation</a>'s resignation from
