his mechanical printer design was not built until 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The first electronic printer was the <a href="/wiki/EP-101" title="EP-101">EP-101</a>, invented by Japanese company <a href="/wiki/Seiko_Epson" title="Seiko Epson">Epson</a> and released in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The first commercial printers generally used mechanisms from <a href="/wiki/Typewriter#Electric_designs" title="Typewriter">electric typewriters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teletype_Corporation" title="Teletype Corporation">Teletype</a> machines. The demand for higher speed led to the development of new systems specifically for computer use. In the 1980s were <a href="/wiki/Daisy_wheel_printing" title="Daisy wheel printing">daisy wheel</a> systems similar to typewriters, <a href="/wiki/Line_printer" title="Line printer">line printers</a> that produced similar output but at much higher speed, and <a href="/wiki/Dot_matrix_printing" title="Dot matrix printing">dot matrix</a> systems that could mix text and graphics but produced relatively low-quality output. The <a href="/wiki/Plotter" title="Plotter">plotter</a> was used for those requiring high quality line art like <a href="/wiki/Blueprint" title="Blueprint">blueprints</a>.</p>/n<p>The introduction of the low-cost laser printer in 1984 with the first <a href="/wiki/HP_LaserJet" title="HP LaserJet">HP LaserJet</a>, and the addition of <a href="/wiki/PostScript" title="PostScript">PostScript</a> in next year's <a href="/wiki/LaserWriter" title="LaserWriter">Apple LaserWriter</a>, set off a revolution in printing known as <a href="/wiki/Desktop_publishing" title="Desktop publishing">desktop publishing</a>. Laser printers using PostScript mixed text and graphics, like dot-matrix printers, but at quality levels formerly available only from commercial <a href="/wiki/Typesetting" title="Typesetting">typesetting</a> systems. By 1990, most simple printing tasks like fliers and brochures were now created on <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computers</a> and then laser printed; expensive <a href="/wiki/Offset_printing" title="Offset printing">offset printing</a> systems were being dumped as scrap. The <a href="/wiki/HP_Deskjet" title="HP Deskjet">HP Deskjet</a> of 1988 offered the same advantages as laser printer in terms of flexibility, but produced somewhat lower quality output (depending on the paper) from much less expensive mechanisms. Inkjet systems rapidly displaced dot matrix and daisy wheel printers from the market. By the 2000s high-quality printers of this sort had fallen under the $100 price point and became commonplace.</p>/n<p>The rapid update of <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">email</a> through the 1990s and into the 2000s has largely displaced the need for printing as a means of moving documents, and a wide variety of reliable storage systems means that a "physical backup" is of little benefit today. Even the desire for printed output for "offline reading" while on mass transit or aircraft has been displaced by <a href="/wiki/E-reader" title="E-reader">e-book readers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet computers</a>. Today, traditional printers are being used more for special purposes, like printing photographs or artwork, and are no longer a must-have peripheral.</p>/n<p>Starting around 2010, <a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printing</a> became an area of intense interest, allowing the creation of physical objects with the same sort of effort as an early laser printer required to produce a brochure. These devices are in their earliest stages of development and have not yet become commonplace.</p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p><i>Personal</i> printers are primarily designed to support individual users, and may be connected to only a single computer. These printers are designed for low-volume, short-turnaround <a href="/wiki/Print_job" title="Print job">print jobs</a>, requiring minimal setup time to produce a hard copy of a given document. However, they are generally slow devices ranging from 6 to around 25 pages per minute (ppm), and the cost per page is relatively high. However, this is offset by the on-demand convenience. Some printers can print documents stored on <a href="/wiki/Memory_card" title="Memory card">memory cards</a> or from <a href="/wiki/Digital_camera" title="Digital camera">digital cameras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Image_scanner" title="Image scanner">scanners</a>.</p>/n<p><i>Networked</i> or <i>shared</i> printers are "designed for high-volume, high-speed printing." They are usually shared by many users on a <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">network</a> and can print at speeds of 45 to around 100 ppm.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Xerox_9700" title="Xerox 9700">Xerox 9700</a> could achieve 120 ppm.</p>/n<p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Virtual_printer" title="Virtual printer">virtual printer</a></i> is a piece of computer software whose user interface and <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">API</a> resembles that of a printer driver, but which is not connected with a physical computer printer. A virtual printer can be used to create a file which is an image of the data which would be printed, for archival purposes or as input to another program, for example to create a <a href="/wiki/PDF" class="mw-redirect" title="PDF">PDF</a> or to transmit to another system or user.</p>/n<p>A <i><a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printer</a></i> is a device for making a three-dimensional object from a 3D model or other electronic data source through additive processes in which successive layers of material (including plastics, metals, food, cement, wood, and other materials) are laid down under computer control. It is called a printer by analogy with an inkjet printer which produces a two-dimensional document by a similar process of depositing a layer of ink on paper.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The choice of print technology has a great effect on the cost of the printer and cost of operation, speed, quality and permanence of documents, and noise. Some printer technologies don't work with certain types of physical media, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_paper" title="Carbon paper">carbon paper</a> or <a href="/wiki/Transparency_(projection)" title="Transparency (projection)">transparencies</a>.</p>/n<p>A second aspect of printer technology that is often forgotten is resistance to alteration: liquid <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">ink</a>, such as from an inkjet head or fabric ribbon, becomes absorbed by the paper fibers, so documents printed with liquid ink are more difficult to alter than documents printed with toner or solid inks, which do not penetrate below the paper surface.</p>/n<p>Cheques can be printed with liquid ink or on special cheque paper with toner anchorage so that alterations may be detected.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The machine-readable lower portion of a cheque must be printed using <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_ink_character_recognition" title="Magnetic ink character recognition">MICR</a> toner or ink. Banks and other clearing houses employ automation equipment that relies on the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">magnetic flux</a> from these specially printed characters to function properly.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_print_technology">Modern print technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern print technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The following <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a> technologies are routinely found in modern printers:</p>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Toner-based_printers">Toner-based printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Toner-based printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Laser_printer" class="mw-redirect" title="Laser printer">Laser printer</a></div>/n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Laser_printing" title="Laser printing">laser printer</a> rapidly produces high quality text and graphics. As with digital <a href="/wiki/Photocopier" title="Photocopier">photocopiers</a> and multifunction printers (MFPs), laser printers employ a <a href="/wiki/Xerography" They use solid sticks of <a href="/wiki/CMYK_color_model" title="CMYK color model">CMYK</a>-coloured ink, similar in consistency to candle wax, which are melted and fed into a piezo crystal operated print-head. The printhead sprays the ink on a rotating, oil coated drum. The paper then passes over the print drum, at which time the image is immediately transferred, or transfixed, to the page. Solid ink printers are most commonly used as colour office printers, and are excellent at printing on transparencies and other non-porous media. Solid ink printers can produce excellent results. Acquisition and operating costs are similar to <a href="/wiki/Laser_printing" title="Laser printing">laser printers</a>. Drawbacks of the technology include high <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy consumption</a> and long warm-up times from a cold state. Also, some users complain that the resulting prints are difficult to write on, as the wax tends to repel inks from <a href="/wiki/Pen" title="Pen">pens</a>, and are difficult to feed through <a href="/wiki/Automatic_document_feeder" title="Automatic document feeder">automatic document feeders</a>, but these traits have been significantly reduced in later models. In addition, this type of printer is only available from one manufacturer, <a href="/wiki/Xerox" title="Xerox">Xerox</a>, manufactured as part of their <a href="/wiki/Xerox_Phaser" title="Xerox Phaser">Xerox Phaser</a> office printer line. Previously, <a href="/wiki/Solid_ink" title="Solid ink">solid ink</a> printers were manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Tektronix" title="Tektronix">Tektronix</a>, but Tek sold the printing business to Xerox in 2001.</p>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dye-sublimation_printers">Dye-sublimation printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dye-sublimation printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dye-sublimation_printer" title="Dye-sublimation printer">Dye-sublimation printer</a></div>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RGB_dye_sublimation_panels.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/RGB_dye_sublimation_panels.jpg/300px-RGB_dye_sublimation_panels.jpg" width="300" height="101" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/RGB_dye_sublimation_panels.jpg/450px-RGB_dye_sublimation_panels.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/RGB_dye_sublimation_panels.jpg/600px-RGB_dye_sublimation_panels.jpg 2x" data-file-width="979" data-file-height="330" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RGB_dye_sublimation_panels.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nA disassembled dye sublimation cartridge</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>A dye-sublimation printer (or dye-sub printer) is a printer which employs a printing process that uses heat to transfer dye to a medium such as a plastic card, paper or <a href="/wiki/Canvas_print" title="Canvas print">canvas</a>. obsolete, or limited to special applications though most were, at one time, in widespread use.</p>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_printers">Impact printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Impact printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<p><span id="Impact_printers"></span><b>Impact printers</b> rely on a forcible impact to transfer ink to the media. The impact printer uses a print head that either hits the surface of the ink ribbon, pressing the ink ribbon against the paper (similar to the action of a <a href="/wiki/Typewriter" title="Typewriter">typewriter</a>), or, less commonly, hits the back of the paper, pressing the paper against the ink ribbon (the <a href="/wiki/IBM_1403" title="IBM 1403">IBM 1403</a> for example). All but the <a href="/wiki/Dot_matrix_printer" class="mw-redirect" title="Dot matrix printer">dot matrix printer</a> rely on the use of <i>fully formed characters</i>, <a href="/wiki/Letterform" title="Letterform">letterforms</a> that represent each of the characters that the printer was capable of printing. In addition, most of these printers were limited to monochrome, or sometimes two-color, printing in a single typeface at one time, although <a href="/wiki/Emphasis_(typography)" title="Emphasis (typography)">bolding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Underlining" class="mw-redirect" title="Underlining">underlining</a> of text could be done by "overstriking", that is, printing two or more impressions either in the same character position or slightly offset. Impact printers varieties include typewriter-derived printers, teletypewriter-derived printers, daisywheel printers, dot matrix printers and line printers. Dot matrix printers remain in common use in businesses where multi-part forms are printed. <i>An overview of impact printing</i><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> contains a detailed description of many of the technologies used.</p>/n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Typewriter-derived_printers">Typewriter-derived printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Typewriter-derived printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>/n<div class="thumb tleft">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:IBM_Selectric_typeball.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/IBM_Selectric_typeball.jpg/220px-IBM_Selectric_typeball.jpg" width="220" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/IBM_Selectric_typeball.jpg/330px-IBM_Selectric_typeball.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/IBM_Selectric_typeball.jpg/440px-IBM_Selectric_typeball.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1413" data-file-height="1362" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:IBM_Selectric_typeball.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/ntypeball print element from IBM Selectric-type printer</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Friden_Flexowriter" title="Friden Flexowriter">Friden Flexowriter</a> and <a href="/wiki/IBM_Selectric_typewriter" title="IBM Selectric typewriter">IBM Selectric typewriter</a></div>/n<p>Several different computer printers were simply computer-controllable versions of existing electric typewriters. The <a href="/wiki/Friden_Flexowriter" title="Friden Flexowriter">Friden Flexowriter</a> and <a href="/wiki/IBM_Selectric_typewriter" title="IBM Selectric typewriter">IBM Selectric-based</a> printers were the most-common examples. The Flexowriter printed with a conventional typebar mechanism while the Selectric used IBM's well-known "golf ball" printing mechanism. In either case, the letter form then struck a ribbon which was pressed against the paper, printing one character at a time. The maximum speed of the Selectric printer (the faster of the two) was 15.5 characters per second.</p>/n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Teletypewriter-derived_printers">Teletypewriter-derived printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Teletypewriter-derived printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Teleprinter" title="Teleprinter">Teleprinter</a></div>/n<p>The common <a href="/wiki/Teleprinter" title="Teleprinter">teleprinter</a> could easily be interfaced to the computer and became very popular except for those computers manufactured by <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>. Some models used a "typebox" that was positioned, in the X- and Y-axes, by a mechanism and the selected letter form was struck by a hammer. Others used a type cylinder in a similar way as the Selectric typewriters used their type ball. In either case, the letter form then struck a ribbon to print the letterform. Most teleprinters operated at ten characters per second although a few achieved 15 CPS.</p>/n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Daisy_wheel_printers">Daisy wheel printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Daisy wheel printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>/n<div class="thumb tleft">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Daisywheel_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Daisywheel_1.jpg/220px-Daisywheel_1.jpg" width="220" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Daisywheel_1.jpg/330px-Daisywheel_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Daisywheel_1.jpg/440px-Daisywheel_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="508" data-file-height="481" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Daisywheel_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/n"daisy wheel" print element</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Daisy_wheel_printer" class="mw-redirect" title="Daisy wheel printer">Daisy wheel printer</a></div>/n<p>Daisy wheel printers operate in much the same fashion as a <a href="/wiki/Typewriter" title="Typewriter">typewriter</a>. A hammer strikes a wheel with petals, the "daisy wheel", each petal containing a letter form at its tip. The letter form strikes a ribbon of <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">ink</a>, depositing the ink on the page and thus printing a character. By rotating the daisy wheel, different characters are selected for printing. These printers were also referred to as <i>letter-quality printers</i> because they could produce text which was as clear and crisp as a typewriter. The fastest letter-quality printers printed at 30 characters per second.</p>/n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Dot-matrix_printers">Dot-matrix printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Dot-matrix printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dot_matrix_printer" class="mw-redirect" title="Dot matrix printer">Dot matrix printer</a></div>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dot_matrix_example_text.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Dot_matrix_example_text.png/220px-Dot_matrix_example_text.png" width="220" height="69" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Dot_matrix_example_text.png/330px-Dot_matrix_example_text.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Dot_matrix_example_text.png/440px-Dot_matrix_example_text.png 2x" data-file-width="727" data-file-height="228" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dot_matrix_example_text.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nSample output from 9-pin dot matrix printer (one character expanded to show detail)</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Dot_matrix_printer" class="mw-redirect" title="Dot matrix printer">dot matrix printer</a> is used for impact printers that use a matrix of small <a href="/wiki/Pin" title="Pin">pins</a> to transfer ink to the page.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The advantage of dot matrix over other impact printers is that they can produce <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphical</a> images in addition to text; however the text is generally of poorer quality than impact printers that use letterforms (<i>type</i>).</p>/n<p>Dot-matrix printers can be broadly divided into two major classes:</p>/n<ul>/n<li>Ballistic wire printers</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Stored_energy_printer" title="Stored energy printer">Stored energy printers</a></li>/n</ul>/n<p>Dot matrix printers can either be <a href="/wiki/Character_(computing)" title="Character (computing)">character</a>-based or line-based (that is, a single horizontal series of pixels across the page), referring to the configuration of the print head.</p>/n<p>In the 1970s &amp; 80s, dot matrix printers were one of the more common types of printers used for general use, such as for home and small office use. Such printers normally had either 9 or 24 pins on the print head (early 7 pin printers also existed, which did not print <a href="/wiki/Descender" title="Descender">descenders</a>). There was a period during the early home computer era when a range of printers were manufactured under many brands such as the <a href="/wiki/Commodore_International" title="Commodore International">Commodore</a> VIC-1525 using the <a href="/wiki/Seikosha" title="Seikosha">Seikosha</a> <b>Uni-Hammer</b> system. This used a single solenoid with an oblique striker that would be actuated 7 times for each column of 7 vertical pixels while the head was moving at a constant speed. The angle of the striker would align the dots vertically even though the head had moved one dot spacing in the time. The vertical dot position was controlled by a synchronised longitudinally ribbed platen behind the paper that rotated rapidly with a rib moving vertically seven dot spacings in the time it took to print one pixel column.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> 24-pin print heads were able to print at a higher quality and started to offer additional type styles and were marketed as <a href="/wiki/Dot_matrix_printing#Near_Letter_Quality_.28NLQ.29" title="Dot matrix printing">Near Letter Quality</a> by some vendors. Once the price of inkjet printers dropped to the point where they were competitive with dot matrix printers, dot matrix printers began to fall out of favour for general use.</p>/n<p>Some dot matrix printers, such as the NEC P6300, can be upgraded to print in colour. This is achieved through the use of a four-colour ribbon mounted on a mechanism (provided in an upgrade kit that replaces the standard black ribbon mechanism after installation) that raises and lowers the ribbons as needed. Colour graphics are generally printed in four passes at standard resolution, thus slowing down printing considerably. As a result, colour graphics can take up to four times longer to print than standard monochrome graphics, or up to 8-16 times as long at high resolution mode.</p>/n<p>Dot matrix printers are still commonly used in low-cost, low-quality applications such as <a href="/wiki/Cash_register" title="Cash register">cash registers</a>, or in demanding, very high volume applications like <a href="/wiki/Invoice" title="Invoice">invoice</a> printing. Impact printing, unlike laser printing, allows the pressure of the print head to be applied to a stack of two or more forms to print <a href="/wiki/Multipart_stationery" title="Multipart stationery">multi-part documents</a> such as sales invoices and <a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">credit card</a> receipts using <a href="/wiki/Continuous_stationery" title="Continuous stationery">continuous stationery</a> with <a href="/wiki/Carbonless_copy_paper" title="Carbonless copy paper">carbonless copy paper</a>. Dot-matrix printers were being superseded even as receipt printers after the end of the twentieth century.</p>/n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Line_printers">Line printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Line printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Line_printer" title="Line printer">Line printer</a></div>/n<p>Line printers print an entire line of text at a time. Four principal designs exist.</p>/n<div class="thumb tleft">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Drum-printer.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Drum-printer.jpg/220px-Drum-printer.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Drum-printer.jpg/330px-Drum-printer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Drum-printer.jpg/440px-Drum-printer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Drum-printer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nPrint drum from drum printer</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li><i>Drum printers</i>, where a horizontally mounted rotating drum carries the entire character set of the printer repeated in each printable character position. The <a href="/wiki/IBM_1132" title="IBM 1132">IBM 1132</a> printer is an example of a drum printer. Drum printers are also found in adding machines and other numeric printers (POS), the dimensions are compact as only a dozen characters need to be supported.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>/n</ul>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PrinterIBM1403_090325.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/PrinterIBM1403_090325.jpg/220px-PrinterIBM1403_090325.jpg" width="220" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/PrinterIBM1403_090325.jpg/330px-PrinterIBM1403_090325.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/PrinterIBM1403_090325.jpg/440px-PrinterIBM1403_090325.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2057" data-file-height="2030" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PrinterIBM1403_090325.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nIBM 1403 line printer</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li><i>Chain or train printers</i>, where the character set is arranged multiple times around a linked chain or a set of character slugs in a track traveling horizontally past the print line. The <a href="/wiki/IBM_1403" title="IBM 1403">IBM 1403</a> is perhaps the most popular, and comes in both chain and train varieties. The <i>band printer</i> is a later variant where the characters are embossed on a flexible steel band. The LP27 from Digital Equipment Corporation is a band printer.</li>/n<li><i>Bar printers</i>, where the character set is attached to a solid bar that moves horizontally along the print line, such as the <a href="/w/index.php?title=IBM_1443&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="IBM 1443 (page does not exist)">IBM 1443</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>/n<li>A fourth design, used mainly on very early printers such as the IBM 402, features independent type bars, one for each printable position. Each bar contains the character set to be printed. The bars moves vertically to position the character to be printed in front of the print hammer.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>/n</ul>/n<p>In each case, to print a line, precisely timed hammers strike against the back of the paper at the exact moment that the correct character to be printed is passing in front of the paper. The paper presses forward against a ribbon which then presses against the character form and the impression of the character form is printed onto the paper.</p>/n<ul>/n<li><i>Comb printers</i>, also called <i><a href="/wiki/Line_matrix_printer" title="Line matrix printer">line matrix printers</a></i>, represent the fifth major design. These printers are a hybrid of <a href="/wiki/Dot_matrix_printing" title="Dot matrix printing">dot matrix printing</a> and line printing. In these printers, a comb of hammers prints a portion of a row of pixels at one time, such as every eighth pixel. By shifting the comb back and forth slightly, the entire pixel row can be printed, continuing the example, in just eight cycles. The paper then advances and the next pixel row is printed. Because far less motion is involved than in a conventional dot matrix printer, these printers are very fast compared to dot matrix printers and are competitive in speed with formed-character line printers while also being able to print dot matrix graphics. The <a href="/wiki/Printronix" title="Printronix">Printronix</a> P7000 series of line matrix printers are still manufactured as of 2013.</li>/n</ul>/n<p>Line printers are the fastest of all impact printers and are used for bulk printing in large computer centres. A line printer can print at 1100 lines per minute or faster, frequently printing pages more rapidly than many current laser printers. On the other hand, the mechanical components of line printers operat with tight tolerances and require regular <a href="/wiki/Preventive_maintenance" title="Preventive maintenance">preventive maintenance</a> (PM) to produce top quality print. They are virtually never used with <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computers</a> and have now been replaced by high-speed <a href="/wiki/Laser_printing" title="Laser printing">laser printers</a>. The legacy of line printers lives on in many computer <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>, which use the abbreviations "lp", "lpr", or "LPT" to refer to printers.</p>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_ink_electrostatic_printers">Liquid ink electrostatic printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Liquid ink electrostatic printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">/n<tr>/n<td class="mbox-image">/n<div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div>/n</td>/n<td class="mbox-text">/n<div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. It was first introduced by Versatec, which was later bought by <a href="/wiki/Xerox" title="Xerox">Xerox</a>. <a href="/wiki/3M" title="3M">3M</a> also used to make these printers.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Plotters">Plotters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Plotters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plotter" title="Plotter">Plotter</a></div>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Calcomp_565_drum_plotter.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Calcomp_565_drum_plotter.jpg/220px-Calcomp_565_drum_plotter.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Calcomp_565_drum_plotter.jpg/330px-Calcomp_565_drum_plotter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Calcomp_565_drum_plotter.jpg/440px-Calcomp_565_drum_plotter.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2048" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Calcomp_565_drum_plotter.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nA Calcomp 565 drum plotter</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>Pen-based <b><a href="/wiki/Plotter" title="Plotter">plotters</a></b> were an alternate printing technology once common in engineering and architectural firms. Pen-based plotters rely on contact with the paper (but not impact, per se) and special purpose pens that are mechanically run over the paper to create text and images. Since the pens output continuous lines, they were able to produce technical drawings of higher resolution than was achievable with dot-matrix technology.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Some plotters used roll-fed paper, and therefore had minimal restriction on the size of the output in one dimension. These plotters were capable of producing quite sizable drawings.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_printers">Other printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Attributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Printer_control_languages">Printer control languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Printer control languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Most printers other than line printers accept <a href="/wiki/Control_character" title="Control character">control characters</a> or unique character sequences to control various printer functions. These may range from shifting from lower to upper case or from black to red ribbon on typewriter printers to switching fonts and changing character sizes and colors on raster printers. Early printer controls were not standardized, with each manufacturer's equipment having its own set. The IBM <a href="/wiki/Personal_Printer_Data_Stream" title="Personal Printer Data Stream">Personal Printer Data Stream</a> (PPDS) became a commonly used command set for dot-matrix printers.</p>/n<p>Today, most printers accept one or more <a href="/wiki/Page_description_language" title="Page description language">page description languages</a> (PDLs). Laser printers with greater processing power frequently offer support for variants of Hewlett-Packard's <a href="/wiki/Printer_Command_Language" title="Printer Command Language">Printer Command Language</a> (PCL), <a href="/wiki/PostScript" title="PostScript">PostScript</a> or <a href="/wiki/XML_Paper_Specification" class="mw-redirect" title="XML Paper Specification">XML Paper Specification</a>. Most inkjet devices support manufacturer proprietary PDLs such as <a href="/wiki/ESC/P" title="ESC/P">ESC/P</a>. The diversity in mobile platforms have led to various standardization efforts around device PDLs such as the <a href="/wiki/Printer_Working_Group" title="Printer Working Group">Printer Working Group</a> (PWG's) <a href="/w/index.php?title=PWG_Raster&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="PWG Raster (page does not exist)">PWG Raster</a>.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Printing_speed">Printing speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Printing speed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The speed of early printers was measured in units of <i>characters per minute</i> (cpm) for character printers, or <i>lines per minute</i> (lpm) for line printers. Modern printers are measured in <i>pages per minute</i> (ppm). These measures are used primarily as a marketing tool, and are not as well standardised as <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_19752" title="ISO/IEC 19752">toner yields</a>. Usually pages per minute refers to sparse monochrome office documents, rather than dense pictures which usually print much more slowly, especially colour images. PPM are most of the time referring to <a href="/wiki/ISO_216" title="ISO 216">A4 paper</a> in Europe and <a href="/wiki/Letter_(paper_size)" title="Letter (paper size)">letter</a> paper in the United States, resulting in a 5-10% difference.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Printing_mode">Printing mode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Printing mode">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The data received by a printer may be:</p>/n<ul>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Plain_text" title="Plain text">A string of characters</a></li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Raster_graphics" title="Raster graphics">A bitmapped image</a></li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Vector_graphics" title="Vector graphics">A vector image</a></li>/n<li>A <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">computer program</a> written in a <a href="/wiki/Page_description_language" title="Page description language">page description language</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Page_description_language" title="Page description language">PCL</a> or <a href="/wiki/PostScript" title="PostScript">PostScript</a></li>/n</ul>/n<p>Some printers can process all four types of data, others not.</p>/n<ul>/n<li>Character printers, such as <a href="/wiki/Daisy_wheel_printer" class="mw-redirect" title="Daisy wheel printer">daisy wheel printers</a>, can handle only plain text data or rather simple point plots.</li>/n<li>Pen <a href="/wiki/Plotter" title="Plotter">plotters</a> typically process <a href="/wiki/Vector_image" class="mw-redirect" title="Vector image">vector images</a>. Inkjet based plotters can adequately reproduce all four.</li>/n<li>Modern printing technology, such as <a href="/wiki/Laser_printing" title="Laser printing">laser printers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inkjet_printer" class="mw-redirect" title="Inkjet printer">inkjet printers</a>, can adequately reproduce all four. This is especially true of printers equipped with support for PCL or PostScript, which includes the vast majority of printers produced today.</li>/n</ul>/n<p>Today it is possible to print everything (even plain text) by sending ready bitmapped images to the printer. This allows better control over formatting, especially among machines from different vendors. Many <a href="/wiki/Printer_driver" title="Printer driver">printer drivers</a> do not use the text mode at all, even if the printer is capable of it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h3><span id="Monochrome,_colour_and_photo_printers"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Monochrome.2C_colour_and_photo_printers">Monochrome, colour and photo printers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Monochrome, colour and photo printers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Monochrome" title="Monochrome">monochrome</a> printer can only produce an <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">image</a> consisting of one <a href="/wiki/Colour" class="mw-redirect" title="Colour">colour</a>, usually black. A monochrome printer may also be able to produce various tones of that color, such as a <a href="/wiki/Grayscale" title="Grayscale">grey-scale</a>. A colour printer can produce images of multiple colours. A photo printer is a colour printer that can produce images that mimic the <a href="/wiki/Gamut" title="Gamut">colour range</a> (gamut) and <a href="/wiki/Image_resolution" title="Image resolution">resolution</a> of prints made from <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">photographic film</a>. Many can be used on a standalone basis without a computer, using a <a href="/wiki/Memory_card" title="Memory card">memory card</a> or <a href="/wiki/Universal_Serial_Bus" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal Serial Bus">USB</a> connector.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Page_yield">Page yield</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Page yield">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The page yield is number of pages that can be printed from a <a href="/wiki/Toner_cartridge" title="Toner cartridge">toner cartridge</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ink_cartridge" title="Ink cartridge">ink cartridge</a>—before the cartridge needs to be refilled or replaced. The actual number of pages yielded by a specific cartridge depends on a number of factors.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>/n<p>For a fair comparison, many laser printer manufacturers use the <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_19752" title="ISO/IEC 19752">ISO/IEC 19752</a> process to measure the toner cartridge yield.<sup id="cite_ref-measuring_yield_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-measuring_yield-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-officeworks_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-officeworks-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_per_page">Cost per page</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Cost per page">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>In order to fairly compare operating expenses of printers with a relatively small <a href="/wiki/Ink_cartridge" title="Ink cartridge">ink cartridge</a> to printers with a larger, more expensive <a href="/wiki/Toner_cartridge" title="Toner cartridge">toner cartridge</a> that typically holds more toner and so prints more pages before the cartridge needs to be replaced, many people prefer to estimate operating expenses in terms of cost per page (CPP).<sup id="cite_ref-measuring_yield_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-measuring_yield-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-officeworks_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-officeworks-21">[21]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_model">Business model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Printer_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Business model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Often the <a href="/wiki/Freebie_marketing" class="mw-redirect" title="Freebie marketing">"razor and blades" business model</a> is applied. That is, a company may sell a printer at cost, and make profits on the <a href="/wiki/Ink_cartridge" title="Ink cartridge">ink cartridge</a>, paper, or some other <a href="/wiki/Spare_part" title="Spare part">replacement part</a>. This has caused legal disputes regarding the right of companies other than the printer manufacturer to sell <a href="/wiki/Computer_compatibility" title="Computer compatibility">compatible</a> ink cartridges. To protect their business model, several manufacturers invest heavily in developing new cartridge technology and patenting it.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Other manufacturers, in reaction to the challenges from using this business model, choose to make more money on printers and less on the ink, promoting the latter through their advertising campaigns. Finally, this generates two clearly different proposals: "cheap printer&#160;– expensive ink" or "expensive printer&#160;– cheap ink". Ultimately, the consumer decision depends on their reference <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rate</a> or their <a href="/wiki/Time_preference" title="Time preference">time preference</a>. From an <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> viewpoint, there is a clear <a href="/wiki/Trade-off" title="Trade-off">trade-off</a> between cost per copy and cost of the printer.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Printer_steganography">Printer steganography</span><span
